The New York Times

As Mobile Product Design Director, I led a team to transform our apps from static feed readers into experiences purpose-built for mobile.

For years, story selection and layouts were determined by editors on the desktop website, then fed downstream to the various mobile apps.

I inherited a suite of first-generation products with little to no variation across cells. Sentences were getting cut off. Headlines and images were uniform.

In time, we got better at interpreting those feeds. It seems trivial now, but in 2013 we were able to collect articles into story groups and vary the weight of headlines according to importance.

While we made incremental improvements to presentation, we also launched a mobile subscription model.

The key was to design a metered experience that acquired paid users without alienating non-subscribers. This was a huge moment in publishing, emulated across the industry.

In 2014, I advocated for a major investment to break the downstream dependence from our desktop website.

I needed to describe what I wanted to do that we couldn't presently do. This design demonstrated how the investment would open up a new generation of editorial styles.

By 2015, what was once an RSS reader was now a dynamic set of presentations designed exclusively for mobile.

Case Study: Discovery

Readers flock to The New York Times for its curated selection of news from editors rather than algorithms. But The Times produces a firehose of great content every day, and only a small portion of it is Top News. How do you surface the remainder?

When we embarked on an iPad app redesign, we turned another feed reader with few visual templates into a stylized experience that amplified the voice and art direction of every section.

As a product director, I pushed to incorporate full covers from the printed magazine for the first time. This required a new feed and additional workflow. A small victory that brought a steady dose of visual intelligence to the top of the app every week.

I also restructured the app so that a broad swathe of content appears on the front page, not just Top News. This distributed readership across the app in an unprecedented way. Viewership of the magazine increased three-fold.

But we still faced the issue of readers feeling like they missed the best material.

So I proposed a sort of Tinder for news that would rely on a strong recommendation engine then under development.

When the recommendation engine didn’t perform as expected, I suggested we pivot to a simpler solution. It made use of two basic pieces of information: an aggregate notion of the most popular stories, and which of those stories a user has seen.

The outcome was In Case You Missed It, a deduped list of trending stories.
