I'm a seasoned UX designer, product strategist, and manager of design teams.

As Mobile Product Design Director at The New York Times, I led a team to transform our offerings from static feed readers, into experiences purpose-built for mobile.

This was the state of our Android app as late as 2013, a downstream reader of the desktop news site.

Crafting a UX around a reversal of this structure was the central narrative of my role at The Times. And we did so in a way that significantly drove digital revenue.

By 2015 we had a suite of award-winning news apps that scaled beautifully across phones and tablets. And more importantly, were no longer dependent on desktop news feeds. Reporters and editors could now publish directly to mobile.

When we embarked on an iPad app redesign, we turned another feed reader with few visual templates into a stylized experience that amplified the voice and art direction of every section.

Problem: Discovery

Readers flock to The New York Times for its handcrafted selection of news, curated by editors rather than algorithms. But that leaves the question of how to surface the remainder. Every day, The Times produces a firehose of great content. Only a small portion of it is Top News.

The very top of a news app is usually a ranked listing of the latest news, weighted according to importance. Users rarely go beyond this surface. The rest of the content is buried under sections.

If we could build an effective recommendation engine, how could it function at the top of our apps?
Rec Engine Designs

Failing a quality recommendation engine. (We didn't always have the luxury of elite engineering talenet.) We did have two simple pieces of information: 1. an aggregate notion of the most popular stories, and 2. which of those stories a user has seen.

Hence, In Case You Missed It. A de-duped list of Trending stories.
